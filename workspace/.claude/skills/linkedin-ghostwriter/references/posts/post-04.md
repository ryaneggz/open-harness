🎙️ I had the pleasure of joining Carlos P. on Advancio Tech-Talks — and we went deep on a topic I'm genuinely passionate about: why traditional software development as we know it is over.

Here's the uncomfortable math most organizations are ignoring:
Developers spend only 8–20% of their time actually writing code. AI coding assistants like GitHub Copilot promise a 30% productivity boost — but 30% of 20% is just 6% overall improvement. That's not transformation. That's a rounding error.
The real problem? We're optimizing the wrong thing.

Most organizations are bolting AI on top of processes built around human limitations from decades ago. That's not AI-native. That's just automation dressed up as innovation.

True AI-native development means: → Rebuilding your entire SDLC around AI — not adding AI to what already exists → Creating reusable organizational vocabularies and prompt systems that compound value across every project → Redefining human roles around what humans do best: intent, innovation, and relationship-building

And perhaps most importantly — this transformation is about people, not technology. Every single role will change. The organizations that get the people side right first will win.

I also share why I give organizations 18 months to cross the chasm — and what happens to those who don't.

Spent today at an AWS workshop on AI-driven SDLC -- and what stood out most wasn’t the tooling… it was the convergence.

What I’ve been seeing firsthand while helping organizations evolve into AI-native engineering teams is now clearly becoming industry pattern.

We’re moving past experimentation. This is about rethinking how software gets built.

A few takeaways (and validations):

• “Vibe coding” has its place -- but not in team-based, enterprise systems. Great for individuals and prototypes. Breaks down quickly when coordination, scale, and reliability matter.

• High code throughput ≠ high engineering productivity. AI can generate more code. But AI-native SDLC is about better systems, faster feedback loops, and higher quality outcomes.

• Productivity gains won’t reduce engineering teams -- they’ll expand ambition. The winners won’t build the same roadmap with fewer engineers. They’ll build significantly more with the same teams.

• The real differentiator is organizational willingness to change. Roles, processes, ownership models -- everything is up for redesign. Teams that embrace this shift will outpace those that layer AI onto legacy workflows.

The tooling will keep evolving (Kiro, Claude Code, GitHub Copilot etc.). But the real shift is structural.

Curious to hear how others are thinking about AI-native SDLC in practice.

We talk about requirements like they're bureaucracy.
Stories in Jira. Documents nobody reads. Acceptance criteria that gets written once and never touched again.

But here's what requirements actually are: a communication tool. The anchor everything else derives from: design, code, tests. The thing that tells us not just how the system works, but how we'll know when it doesn't.

In the AI era, that anchor is dragging.
AI helps you move fast. You generate implementation, see results, learn something new - all in minutes. But everyone else does the same thing. If we're not updating our acceptance criteria coherently, it's a communication problem waiting to happen.

Here's a simple test: how often does your team go back and update the AC after learning something from the build?

Once a sprint? Once a quarter? Never?

That gap between what you specified and what you're actually learning is called the Intent Gap. And it's one of the monsters I covered in last month's webinar.

On April 15th we're going deeper. Same monsters, in real API testing scenarios. And how we close the intent gap.
